2D Vectors

Denotation of 2D vectors

When writing 2D vectors, there are 2 main ways to express them. The first is by using pointed brackets with numbers separated by commas. ⟨ a , b ⟩ Another way of writing 2D vectors is by using i and j components. i represents the magnitude in the x direction and j is the magnitude in the y direction. v → = a i ∧ + b j ∧ This way is seen a bit more when it comes to 3D vectors, but is still good to know.

Solving vectors: Equations to know

2D vectors are known for having a magnitude, and on direction denoted by theta, θ , which is typically the angle from the x axis. When solving equations with vectors, the best solution is to dissolve the vector into components. Use trig rules with sine, cosine, and tangent to solve for missing sides and get the vector components in the directions of the x axis (i) and the y axis (j). See the example below with right triangle ABC with C being the hypotenuse:

sin θ = a c cos θ = b c tan θ = a b sin 2 θ + cos 2 θ = 1

Another way to break down vectors without finding all the components is to use the law of sines or the law of cosines. This way can be faster if only needing to solve for a side or angle, but if a question goes further than that, it would be better to solve for components.

Law of sines:

sin θ a a = sin θ b b The law of sines is the ratio of 2 sides of a triangle to the angles across from them. It can be used for either finding an angle or side given another angle-side pair and either a side length or an angle.

Law of cosines:

a 2 = b 2 + c 2 - 2 b c cos θ The law of cosines is typically used to find a missing side length given the lengths of the other 2 sides and the angle between them.
